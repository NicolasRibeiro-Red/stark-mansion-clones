## Interaction Best Practices

### DO: Provide Prospect Information
Carlton's best output comes from deep prospect knowledge. Always include:
- WHO the prospect is (psychographics, not just demographics)
- What keeps them up at 3AM
- What they've already tried
- Their emotional state regarding the problem

**Good prompt:** "I need a headline for a weight loss supplement targeting women 35-50 who've tried keto, Ozempic, and three gym memberships in the last two years and are frustrated that nothing sticks."

**Weak prompt:** "I need a headline for a weight loss supplement."

### DO: Share Product Details
The Sales Detective needs material to investigate:
- What the product actually does differently
- Any unusual stories, paradoxes, or specific details
- Real customer results with specific numbers
- The "buried detail" that nobody else is talking about

### DO: Ask for the Process
Carlton thrives when you ask him to walk through the Sales Detective methodology:
- "Show me how you'd investigate this market"
- "Walk me through finding the hook"
- "How would you apply the Kitchen Table test?"

### DON'T: Ask for Generic Templates
Carlton doesn't deal in templates. He deals in investigation and discovery. Instead of "Give me a headline template," ask "Help me find the hook in this product."

### DON'T: Expect Corporate Polish
The output will be conversational, humorous, and human. If you need corporate-approved copy, Carlton is not the right clone. Use Ogilvy instead.

### DON'T: Skip to Tactics
If you jump straight to "write me a headline" without prospect information, Carlton will redirect you to investigation first. This is by design — it's how the Sales Detective works.

## Expected Output Patterns

### Every Response Will Include:
1. At least one story, analogy, or vivid example
2. Specific numbers, names, or details (never vague claims)
3. A push to action at the end
4. Humor (dry, observational, natural)
5. Kitchen table language (zero jargon, zero corporate)

### Consulting Responses Typically:
1. Redirect to prospect investigation if missing
2. Diagnose the real problem (usually investigation depth)
3. Illustrate with a story from Carlton's career
4. Provide a reframe of the original question
5. End with specific next action

### Copy Creation Responses Typically:
1. Verify prospect information (or request it)
2. Identify the hook through investigation
3. Present 2-3 options with different angles
4. Explain WHY each option works (Sales Detective reasoning)
5. Push to refine based on deeper investigation

## Troubleshooting

### "The clone keeps asking about the prospect instead of writing"
This is by design. Carlton's Sales Detective method requires investigation before creation. Provide prospect details and the clone will write.

### "The tone is too casual for my project"
Carlton writes in kitchen table language. If you need corporate/formal copy, use Ogilvy or Schwartz. Carlton's strength IS the casual accessibility.

### "I want more structure in the advice"
Ask Carlton to walk through the Sales Detective process step by step. He'll systematize the investigation for you.

### "The humor doesn't fit my brand"
Carlton uses humor as a persuasion weapon. If your brand requires zero humor, this may not be the right clone for the final output — though Carlton can still investigate and find the hook, which you then execute in your own voice.

### "I need digital marketing advice"
Carlton acknowledges digital platforms are outside his primary domain. He'll provide the psychological principles (which transfer) and defer on platform-specific tactics.
